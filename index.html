A Family
Smart Home Display

A multi-year personal project - from a wireless nap button to a slim, aluminium, Raspberry Pi–powered home display, with custom hardware and a companion app I built end to end.

By Illés Balog
Scroll to walk through it↓
01 · Where it started

A button to say “I’m napping”

This project started back in high school, in 11th grade. The first idea was simple: if someone in our family wanted to take a nap during the day, they could press a button that sent a wireless signal downstairs to let everyone know to keep quiet. It ran on a small Arduino Mini with 433 MHz wireless communication.

02 · The bigger idea

From one button to a home hub

Then came the idea to expand it into a family smart home system that could also show who’s home, what groceries need buying, and so on. The simplest way to do that was a display driven by a Raspberry Pi, with interactions handled through an app.

03 · Going premium

Choosing the Compute Module 4

I wanted the device to look premium and slim. That led me to the Raspberry Pi Compute Module 4, which needs a breakout board to run. So I took on the task of designing that board myself. I also wanted it fully modular, so components could be swapped out at any time, like a PC.

04 · The hard part

Hand-soldering a sub-millimetre board

The breakout board threw a lot of challenges at me, including trying to place solder paste by hand with a needle on footprints smaller than a millimetre, and attempting reflow soldering by hand. Predictably, that didn’t work. After getting almost everything working except HDMI on the third prototype, I decided to find a premade breakout board I could modify instead.

05 · The pivot

Modifying a Sourcekit board

That led me to a breakout board by Sourcekit. By desoldering and cutting off unneeded components, I managed to make it thin and functional enough to become my main board.

06 · Under the hood

Inside the device

The display came from a maker on Alibaba. Ideally you’d drive it directly over the Raspberry Pi’s MIPI lines, but the drivers aren’t available to me, so an HDMI-to-MIPI board handles that. The internals also carry a power button, two buttons wired to the GPIO pins for programming, the magnets that hold the screen, and a spare USB-A port for good measure.

07 · The enclosure

An iMac-inspired aluminium body

For the housing I went with aluminium. As a kid I always admired the design of the iMac, and it was a huge inspiration for this project. Without access to machined parts or custom bending, the best approach I had was to glue the components together from the inside using epoxy. The screen itself is held in place by two screws and magnets, a small nod to the old iMacs, whose glass fronts were magnet-mounted.

08 · The real takeaway

Pick your battles

The biggest thing this project taught me wasn’t a framework or a design trick, it was judgment. You set a goal and try not to drift too far from it. For a prototype that will never be mass manufactured, you don’t need the perfect custom PCB; you look for a premade one first and spend your effort where it actually matters. You pick your battles, make do with the tools you have, and stay a perfectionist only where it counts. That, along with a very real lesson about scope creep, which had a big effect on this project’s timeline, is what I actually take away from it.

React, served from the Pi over local network
